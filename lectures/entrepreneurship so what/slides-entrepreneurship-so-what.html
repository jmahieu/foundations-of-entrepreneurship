What is entrepreneurship?

How we define entrepreneurship

Entrepreneurship is the pursuit of opportunity beyond resources controlled.

— Howard Stevenson
  • Focus on decision making under uncertainty with limited resources.
E(V) = P × V … but P (probability of success) and V (value of a successful project) are both unknown up front.
  • Should I pursue an opportunity (or not)? How can I learn about its value?
  • Which choices do I need to make to realise the potential of this opportunity?

Case: Jibo

Why does Jibo fit our definition of entrepreneurship?

  • The potential value of an idea (opportunity) — and how to realise it — is uncertain up front.
  • There is more than one path to create and capture value from this idea.
  • Only through experimentation can entrepreneurs learn the true value of an idea and how to realise its potential.
  • Experimentation is costly and entrepreneurs have only limited resources — many need external resources.

The entrepreneurial catch-22

  • Uncertainty and risk can only be reduced by having resources.
  • Resource providers might only commit when uncertainty and risk are low.
  • Traditional business strategies and tactics break down.

The questions this raises

Part 1 of the course
  • Who becomes an entrepreneur and why, when the odds of success are unknown but likely very low?
  • What are the private and social returns to entrepreneurship?
  • Which individual and contextual factors impact entrepreneurial performance?
Part 2 of the course
  • How do entrepreneurs form their initial beliefs about opportunities?
  • How do they learn in an uncertain environment with limited resources to experiment?
  • How do they ultimately persuade others?

What is an entrepreneur (according to the GEM)?

Entrepreneur
An adult who is engaged in setting up or operating a new venture that is ≤ 42 months old.
TEA rate
The percentage of the 18–64 population who are either a nascent entrepreneur or an owner-manager of a new business.

Problems with using new venture creation as definition of entrepreneurship

1

Many new ventures are mundane, hobby businesses that generate little private or social value — “subsistence” rather than “transformational” entrepreneurship.

2

A focus on new firms (≤ 3.5 years) excludes the growth and exit stages of entrepreneurship.

3

Excludes people who are in the entrepreneurial process but never reach the stage of actually setting up a business (survival bias) — roughly 50% of everyone who considers starting a business (Bennett & Chatterji, 2023).

Bennett, V. M., & Chatterji, A. K. (2023). The entrepreneurial process: Evidence from a nationally representative survey. Strategic Management Journal, 44(1), 86–116. https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.3077

Is entrepreneurship = self-employment?

  • Self-employment captures the income risk-taking aspect of entrepreneurship.
  • Easy to track — a measure available at the individual level, in administrative data and labour-force surveys.

Is entrepreneurship = self-employment?

  • False self-employment — someone carries out an assignment as a self-employed professional but is in reality an employee — e.g. they cannot decide themselves when or how to do the work. The client is effectively the employer but pays no payroll taxes.
  • Management companies (Belgium) — holdings set up mainly by high-income individuals so income is taxed as corporate earnings instead of as wages (and many purchases can be deducted as operating costs).

Productive, unproductive, and destructive entrepreneurship

“The basic hypothesis is that, while the total supply of entrepreneurs varies among societies, the productive contribution of the society’s entrepreneurial activities varies much more because of their allocation between productive activities such as innovation and largely unproductive activities such as rent seeking or organized crime. This allocation is heavily influenced by the relative payoffs society offers to such activities.”
— Baumol (1990), Journal of Political Economy 98(5), 893–921

Do subsidies spur productive or unproductive entrepreneurship?

“We document a null effect of [local public programs targeting innovative start-ups] over a wide range of firm-level outcomes. However, we find that securing local subsidies increases start-ups’ probability to obtain additional public subsidies, which points in the direction of a vicious ‘Matthew effect’ in subsidy allocation.”
— Santoleri & Russo (2025), Research Policy 54(1)

In a nutshell: measuring entrepreneurship is hard

  • Entrepreneurship is a wide spectrum — from bootstrapped software apps to VC-backed deep-tech start-ups in the space industry.
  • Operationalising all the distinctive characteristics (uncertainty, limited resources, experimentation) at once is very challenging at scale.
  • Each definition is a trade-off:
    • Restricting to start-ups with an innovative product leaves out entrepreneurs who tried to create something innovative but failed along the way.
  • Keep these trade-offs in mind throughout the course — in your own research, when reading policy reports, in the news, …

Why should we care about entrepreneurship?

Startups contribute most to productivity growth

Source: Alon, Berger, Dent & Pugsley (2018), “Older and slower: The startup deficit’s lasting effects on aggregate productivity growth”, Journal of Monetary Economics 93, 68–85.

Startups create (and destroy) most jobs

Source: Haltiwanger, Jarmin, Kulick & Miranda (2016), “High-growth young firms”, in Measuring Entrepreneurial Businesses, NBER / University of Chicago Press, 11–62.

… but only a small fraction of startups create all those jobs

Startups disproportionately contribute to breakthrough innovations
